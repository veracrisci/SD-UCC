Service & Process Design - UCC Program

Voice

Replacing legacy voice infrastructure while preserving the traveller and agent experience, starting with the Hungary pilot

Vera Crisci (Service Design) | June 2026

Track Scope

What this track covers

The Voice track migrates ABU voice service from legacy platforms (Cisco) to AWS Connect. Unlike a new channel, this is a replacement of something travellers already rely on. Expectations are already set, and any regression in the experience is immediately felt.

Hungary is the first launch: one market, one site, used to validate the platform, contact flows and operational model before scaling further.

SPD focus: The service journeys are already established, though there is room for improvement in the IVR. Contact flows exist but are being adapted to enable the pilot launch (they must return to their integrity post-pilot). The risk sits on both sides: introducing something new that does not reflect what travellers had before, or carrying over what existed without questioning whether it still works well enough.

Key attributes

  • Platform migration (Cisco to AWS Connect)
  • Existing traveller expectations - any degradation is immediately visible
  • IVR and contact flow redesign opportunity
  • Hungary pilot as bounded first launch
  • Legacy decommissioning dependency (Cisco EOL)

Experience Assessment

Key experience observations

Design focus

Contact flow design

The IVR and contact flow is the traveller's first experience of the new platform. How it is designed determines whether the entry feels intelligent (IFD principle) or impersonal. SPD designed the Hungary pilot flows, treating the IVR as a traveller touchpoint rather than a technical configuration.

Delivering well

IVR testing thoroughness

Testing surfaced 19+ IVR and call transfer bugs during the pilot preparation phase, all of which have been resolved. Issues ranged from incorrect flow paths (cancellation routing reversed) to missing prompts and transfer failures. The fact that these were found and fixed before go-live is a sign of rigorous validation.

Needs attention

Context handover at routing

The journey design defines how intent and context gathered in the IVR should transfer to the agent. The integrations that deliver this (reservation info, skip the line) are still in testing and not yet confirmed working end-to-end. Until they are validated, there is a risk that agents start calls without the context the design intended them to have.

Needs attention

Unified agent desktop blocked

The ticket for agents accessing a single desktop for Voice and Live Chat is currently blocked. Without this, agents handling both channels will switch between tools rather than working from one unified view. This affects handle time, context retention and the ability to see a traveller's full interaction history across channels.

Needs attention

Legacy parity expectation

Travellers expect at minimum the same service they get today. The Wave 1 rollout plan explicitly describes each language as "replicating the pilot configuration baseline." This assumption that replication is sufficient has not been validated from an experience perspective. Six language rollouts have already been cancelled with no stated rationale.

Needs attention

No experience measurement defined

There are no tickets in the backlog for voice CSAT, quality monitoring or experience measurement. The program can track whether the migration happened but not whether the experience improved or degraded. Without baseline metrics before go-live, there is no way to assess the pilot's impact on traveller satisfaction.
